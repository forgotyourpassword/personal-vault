## Source thought

While listening to The Knowledge Project episode published May 12, 2026 with Winston Weinberg, cofounder of Harvey AI, Michael picked up this leadership note: building a company means having 1,000 failures and only a few successes. You have to fail a lot in order to win big a few times.

## Why this is useful

This is a good founder/leader operating truth: the job is not to avoid failure, but to create enough intelligent attempts that the few major wins have room to appear. The emotional trap is treating each miss as evidence that the strategy is broken. The better frame is portfolio thinking: many small failures are the cost of finding the few asymmetric successes that matter.

## Takeaways

- Company building is a high-failure-rate game by nature.
- The goal is not a perfect batting average. It is enough high-quality swings to produce a few outsized wins.
- Leaders need to normalize failed attempts without normalizing sloppy execution.
- The useful question after failure is not “how do we avoid failing?” but “was this a good bet, did we learn fast, and what changes before the next swing?”
- Morale depends on helping the team understand that the failures are not the opposite of success. They are usually the admission price.

## Harbor View leadership application

For Harbor View, this is a reminder to judge experiments by learning velocity and upside, not by whether every initiative lands immediately. New service lines, AI workflows, client pitches, team roles, and internal systems will not all work. The company needs a culture where smart failures are mined quickly, bad failures are corrected honestly, and the few things that work get doubled down on hard.

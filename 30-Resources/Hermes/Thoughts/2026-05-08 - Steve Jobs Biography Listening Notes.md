## 2026-05-08 - Early Apple, ambition, relationships, and reality distortion

### Source thought
Michael noticed that Steve Jobs was intensely focused on making money and succeeding, and that this helped him build momentum, but it also led to early decisions that burned personal relationships with people who had trusted him. One early contributor who helped while Apple was still operating out of a garage received essentially nothing when the company went public, despite many others receiving shares or stock options that later became valuable in the IPO.

In the early Apple years, people described Jobs as temperamental. He would cry, yell, and insult people. As the Mac division began seeing success, leadership turmoil became a real problem. Apple replaced a CEO after that person handled layoffs poorly and mismanaged the team.

Jobs was also described as having a “reality distortion field”: in conversation, he could convince people of almost anything, even if it did not line up with what could actually be done. The effect sometimes wore off after people left the room, leaving teams with timelines and expectations that seemed unreasonable. But the upside was that he could push people to change the status quo and bend practical reality further than they believed possible.

One example: an engineer said a mouse capable of moving across all axes could not be done. Jobs fired him on the spot and hired a replacement who said he thought it could be done. Jobs was deeply unwilling to work with people who did not believe amazing things were possible, which made him both a force multiplier and a ruthless leader.

### Why this is useful
This is a clean founder tradeoff: Jobs’ intensity, persuasion, and refusal to accept conventional limits helped produce extraordinary products, but the same traits created collateral damage in trust, fairness, retention, and leadership stability. The lesson is not “be like Jobs.” It is more precise: high standards and reality-bending ambition can create breakthroughs, but if they are not constrained by ethics and relationship discipline, they become extractive.

### Takeaways
- Ambition compounds when paired with taste and conviction, but it can corrode trust if people feel used.
- Equity and early contributor treatment are not administrative details. They become moral history.
- “Reality distortion” can be useful when it expands what people attempt, dangerous when it ignores capacity, timelines, or human cost.
- Refusing to work with people who say “impossible” can raise the ceiling, but firing disbelief on sight is a blunt instrument.
- Jobs’ leadership power came with a real shadow: he could inspire people past their limits, and sometimes past what was fair.

# The Armor Building Formula - Review and Fitness Application

**Book:** [[The Armor Building Formula - Index|The Armor Building Formula]]  
**Author:** Dan John  
**Reviewed:** 2026-05-28  
**Source PDF:** [[Dan John - The Armor Building Formula.pdf]]

## One-line take

This is a strong fit for Michael right now because it favors simple, repeatable strength work over complex programming, and it pairs well with rebuilding running, hiking, rucking, and general fitness.

## What the book is really saying

Dan John's core formula is brutally simple:

- Lift weights.
- Eat protein.
- Walk.
- Use enough volume to grow, then add load, then occasionally tighten rest or time.
- Do not turn every workout into a heroic event.

The kettlebell center of gravity is the Armor Building Complex:

- 2 cleans
- 1 press
- 3 front squats

The book then builds around pressing volume, ABC rounds, walking after training, and a long-term progression model of volume → load/intensity → density.

## Useful principles for Michael

### 1. This should replace some strength complexity, not add more noise

Your existing fitness system already has running, biking, bodyweight work, rings, bands, ankle mobility, and general strength. The mistake would be stapling Dan John's program on top of all of that.

Better move: use Armor Building as the default strength pattern for 6-8 weeks, while keeping running/base work easy.

### 2. The book fits your current constraints

Current vault notes say:

- Goal: lose 5 lb
- Goal: rebuild running base
- Goal: gain overall strength and toned upper body
- Preferred schedule: 4 flexible morning sessions/week
- Coming off roughly 7 months low activity
- Available equipment includes dumbbells, weighted vest, rings, bands, punching bag, Echelon bike, Garmin
- You logged a 35 lb front kettlebell squat with press recently, so the movement family is already in orbit

That means the best version for you is not maximal mass-building. It is a "stronger, leaner, more durable" version.

### 3. Walking is not filler

Dan John treats post-lift walking as part of the training effect: fat loss support, recovery, transition back to life, and low-risk volume. For you, this maps well to walks, hikes, rucks, and easy outdoor time.

### 4. Progress one lever at a time

The book's best long-term idea is volume → load → density. Do not try to add reps, add load, and reduce rest all at once. That is how joints file a complaint with HR.

For the next block, progression should mostly be volume:

- More clean/press/squat rounds
- More total press reps
- Same load until it feels controlled
- Only then heavier bells/dumbbells or tighter timing

## Recommended incorporation

### Best starting plan: 6-week Armor Lite block

Use this as a bridge between your current restart phase and harder training later.

Weekly structure:

- Day 1: Armor Strength A
- Day 2: Easy run or bike, zone 2
- Day 3: Armor Strength B
- Day 4: Long easy run, hike, or ruck

Keep your existing ankle mobility in every warm-up.

### Armor Strength A: press emphasis

Warm-up:

- Ankle circles, calf/ankle mobility, leg swings
- Band pull-aparts or light rows
- 5 minutes easy bike/walk

Main:

- Clean and press pattern: 2-3-5 ladder, 2-3 rounds to start
- Goblet squat or front squat: 3 x 8-10
- Ring rows: 3 x 8-12
- Push-ups or ring dips: 2-3 easy sets, stop before grind

Cool-down:

- 20-30 minute walk if schedule allows
- If not, 10 minutes minimum

### Armor Strength B: complex emphasis

Warm-up:

- Same ankle/mobility warm-up
- A few light clean/press/squat practice reps

Main:

- Armor Building Complex variation: start with 5-8 quality rounds
- Rest as needed, no racing yet
- Add 1-2 rounds per week if recovery is good
- Optional finisher: curls, loaded carry, or band pull-aparts, but only if it does not steal recovery from running

Cool-down:

- Walk 20-30 minutes, or easy Echelon spin if weather/work blocks the walk

### If you only have one 35 lb kettlebell/dumbbell available

Use Dan John's single-bell idea instead of forcing a double-bell program:

- Left clean and press
- Switch
- Right clean and press
- Two front squats
- Put it down, shake out, repeat starting right side next round

Start with 8-10 rounds, not 30. Earn boring consistency first.

## What I would change in your current Weekly Training Plan

Do not overhaul the whole thing. Replace the two current strength sessions with Armor-style sessions for one block:

- Strength A becomes press ladder + squat + row
- Strength B becomes ABC rounds + pull/core accessory
- Keep easy run and long/easy run exactly as-is
- Keep bike as active recovery

This preserves your 4-day rhythm and gives the program room to work.

## Guardrails

- No density chasing for the first 3-4 weeks.
- No grinding presses. Shoulders need consistency, not ego reps.
- Keep running easy during the first two weeks while legs adapt to squat volume.
- If soreness is high, cut ABC rounds before cutting running habit.
- Protein matters. The book's plain-language version is correct: lift weights and eat protein. For you, make protein the default at the next meal after strength sessions.

## Suggested tracking metrics

Add these to [[Fitness Progress]] if you run the block:

- ABC rounds completed
- Press ladder total reps
- Load used
- Walk duration after lifting
- Next-day soreness: 1-5
- Running still happened this week: yes/no

## My recommendation

Run this as a 6-week experiment, not a life identity. The win condition is:

- 2 strength sessions/week completed consistently
- 2 easy aerobic sessions/week maintained
- ABC rounds climb gradually without wrecking your legs
- Pressing volume improves
- Body feels more athletic, not more beaten up

If it works, repeat with slightly heavier load or a true 8-week Dan John block. If it interferes with running or makes you sore enough to skip sessions, it is too much too soon. The program is supposed to be reasonable. Let it be reasonable.

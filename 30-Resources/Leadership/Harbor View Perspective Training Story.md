# The Same Storm — A Harbor View Consulting Training Story

*A fictional training narrative on perspective, circumstance, and professional relationships*

---

## The Setup

Harbor View Consulting had just landed its biggest client of the year — a regional healthcare network looking to overhaul its operational processes across seven facilities. It was a twelve-month engagement, high visibility, and the kind of work that could define careers.

Two senior consultants were assigned as co-leads: **Marcus** and **Dana**.

Same project. Same client. Same team. Same timeline. Same budget. Same manager.

Same storm.

---

## The First Test: The Scope Creep

Six weeks in, the client expanded their ask. What started as a process audit had quietly grown into a full technology recommendation and a change management rollout — work that was well outside the original scope and hadn't been priced into the contract.

Marcus found out in a Friday afternoon email from the client's VP. He didn't get a heads-up from anyone at Harbor View first.

Dana found out the same way, same email, same Friday afternoon.

---

**Marcus read it and felt his stomach drop.**

No one told him. He'd been kept out of the loop. The client was walking all over the engagement, and clearly no one at Harbor View had his back. His manager probably knew and said nothing. The client was taking advantage. He'd been set up to fail.

He spent the weekend stewing. By Monday he came in guarded, short in his responses to the client, and pulled back from proactively sharing updates with his manager — if they weren't going to loop him in, he wasn't going to go out of his way either.

In the next client meeting, the VP sensed the chill. She didn't know the source, but she noted it. She started cc'ing Dana more than Marcus on her emails. She found Marcus harder to read and began routing questions around him.

Marcus noticed. Which confirmed what he already believed: no one had his back.

---

**Dana read the same email and felt the pressure — but she paused before reacting.**

She didn't have all the information. The client's VP was probably getting pulled in ten directions. Maybe she'd already started talking to someone else at Harbor View and Dana just hadn't been looped yet. Maybe this was an oversight. Maybe it was a real problem that needed to be addressed — but either way, getting reactive on a Friday wasn't going to help anyone.

She sent a quick note to her manager: "Hey — saw the VP's email about the expanded scope. I want to make sure we're aligned before the week kicks off. Got 20 minutes Monday morning?"

Her manager, who'd been trying to figure out how to have exactly that conversation, replied within the hour: "Yes — was just about to reach out. Good catch."

Monday morning Dana walked into that conversation with context, a seat at the table, and the reputation of someone who raised concerns cleanly rather than quietly punishing people for them.

---

## The Second Test: The Team Member

Midway through the engagement, both Marcus and Dana were working closely with a junior analyst named Priya. She was sharp, but new — and under pressure, she had a tendency to go quiet rather than flag problems early.

In week fourteen, Priya missed a key deliverable. A data summary that both consultants were counting on for a client presentation wasn't ready. No warning. No explanation until the morning it was due.

---

**Marcus was furious.**

He'd trusted her with that piece and she'd let him down. In the debrief, he was visibly impatient. He made it clear he was disappointed. He assigned her a smaller role going forward — not a formal demotion, just quietly reducing her responsibilities. He told himself it was about managing risk.

Priya shut down. She stopped bringing ideas to the team. She did exactly what she was asked and nothing more. She stopped flagging problems early — which, ironically, made the risk worse.

---

**Dana was frustrated too — but she was curious before she was judgmental.**

She pulled Priya aside privately. "Walk me through what happened. I'm not looking to assign blame — I just want to understand what got in the way so we can keep it from happening again."

What she learned: Priya had hit a data access issue on day two of the task and hadn't wanted to look like she couldn't handle it. She'd spent a week trying to solve it herself before running out of time.

Dana acknowledged the mistake clearly — she didn't excuse it — but she also gave Priya a framework for escalating early and made it clear that asking for help was a strength, not a weakness.

Two weeks later, Priya flagged a potential data discrepancy in a client report before it went out the door. It would have been embarrassing in front of a C-suite audience. Dana gave her the credit publicly. Priya became one of the most proactive people on the team.

---

## The Third Test: The Setback

In month nine, a key recommendation the team had built the engagement around — a process redesign for the client's billing department — got rejected by the client's CFO. Not adjusted. Rejected. The CFO had concerns that had apparently been there for months, but no one had surfaced them.

Both consultants had invested heavily in that work. It stung.

---

**Marcus took it as evidence that the whole engagement was rigged.** The client never really wanted independent recommendations. They just wanted someone to validate what they'd already decided. All that work, for nothing. He started mentally checking out, doing what was required and nothing more, counting down the months.

In the final client survey, he received feedback that he was professional but "disengaged in the back half of the project."

Harbor View's leadership noted it.

---

**Dana was disappointed, but she asked a different question: what can we learn from this?**

She requested a one-hour conversation with the CFO — not to relitigate the recommendation, but to understand his concerns. He was surprised she asked. He'd braced for pushback.

In that conversation, she learned that the billing team had tried a similar redesign four years ago and it had gone badly. The CFO had scar tissue she didn't know about. His resistance wasn't irrational — it was institutional memory.

Dana revised the recommendation with that context built in. She addressed the historical failure directly. She gave the CFO a version he could bring to his team without feeling like he was making the same mistake twice.

The revised recommendation was approved. The client extended the engagement by three months.

Dana was asked to lead the follow-on project.

Marcus was not asked back.

---

## Same Storm. Different Outcomes.

Marcus and Dana faced the same scope creep, the same underperforming team member, the same rejection. On paper, they had equal talent, equal access, equal resources.

The difference was never the circumstance.

It was what each of them believed the circumstance meant — and what they believed about the people around them.

Marcus experienced every setback as evidence of a system working against him. That belief shaped every decision he made: how he communicated, how he trusted, how he invested. The belief created the reality he feared.

Dana experienced every setback as information. She stayed curious about people's motivations rather than assuming the worst. She extended trust while staying sharp. She brought people toward her instead of pushing them away.

Neither person changed the storm. One of them learned to move through it differently.

---

## Discussion Questions

1. Have you ever been in a Marcus moment — where a situation felt like it was being done to you rather than something you were moving through? What shifted your perspective, if anything?

2. Think of a time you assumed the worst about a colleague's intentions and were wrong. What context did you eventually get that changed your interpretation?

3. What is one relationship in your professional life where extending more charitable interpretation could change the dynamic?

4. Dana asked for conversations that most people avoid. What makes that difficult, and what makes it worth it?

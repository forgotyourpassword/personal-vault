# AI Governance

## Current State
Governance preparedness has improved to 30% highly prepared (up +6pp from prior year), but most organizations still treat it as compliance rather than a strategic capability. Only 21% have a mature governance model for autonomous agents (see [[agentic-ai-adoption]]).

## Top AI Risks
1. **Data privacy and/or security** — 73%
2. **Legal, IP, or regulatory compliance** — 50%
3. **Governance capabilities and oversight** — 46%
4. **Model quality, consistency, and explainability** — 46%
5. **Workforce impact** — 30%

## The Governance Problem
As reported in the Deloitte survey, some organizations have no clear inventory of active AI tools/models because development occurred without systematic tracking or centralized visibility.

## Principles for Effective Governance
- **Governance as catalyst, not friction** — it enables rapid, confident scaling when done right
- **Senior leadership must actively shape governance** — organizations delegating to technical teams alone achieve less business value
- **Cross-functional teams** — IT, legal, compliance, and business unit leaders together
- **Integrate into existing risk structures** — not parallel "shadow" governance functions
- **Define human-in-the-loop boundaries** — which decisions agents make independently vs require human approval
- **Real-time monitoring and audit trails** — track agent behavior, flag anomalies, capture full chain of actions for accountability
- **Balance risk with innovation** — oversight should enable experimentation, not constrain it

## Autonomous Agent Governance
Unlike conventional AI that provides recommendations, autonomous agents take actions directly — making purchases, sending communications, modifying systems. This requires:
- Clear boundaries for agent autonomy
- Real-time monitoring systems
- Comprehensive audit trails
- Cross-functional governance structures
- Start with lower-risk use cases, build governance capabilities, then scale

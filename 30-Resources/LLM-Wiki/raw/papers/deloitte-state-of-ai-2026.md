# State of AI in the Enterprise — The Untapped Edge

**Source:** Deloitte AI Institute
**Date:** January 2026
**Survey:** 3,235 director-to-C-suite leaders across 6 industries and 24 countries
**Field dates:** August–September 2025
**URL:** deloitte.com/us/state-of-ai
**Augmented with:** 15 interviews with global C-suite executives and AI/data science leaders

## Industries surveyed
- Consumer
- Energy, resources and industrials
- Financial services
- Life sciences and health care
- Technology, media and telecom
- Government and public services

## Key Findings

### AI Access and Pilot Expansion
- Worker access to sanctioned AI tools grew from <40% to ~60% in one year (50% increase)
- 11% of leading companies provide >80% worker access to AI tools
- Among workers with access, <60% use AI in daily workflow (unchanged from prior year)
- 25% of companies have moved 40%+ of AI experiments to production to date
- 54% expect to reach that 40%+ production level within next 3–6 months

### The Proof-of-Concet Trap
- Pilots run with small teams, cleansed data, isolated environments (months)
- Production requires: infrastructure investment, system integration, security reviews, compliance checks, monitoring, ongoing maintenance
- Use cases estimated for 3 months stretch to 18 months when integration complexity emerges
- "Pilot fatigue" — funding new pilots (low cost/risk) rather than scaling existing successes
- "If there is no coherent AI strategy... you're likely to see pilot fatigue"

### AI Transformation Impact
- 25% report AI having transformative effect (up from 12% a year ago, more than double)
- 84% increasing AI investment
- 78% leaders report greater confidence in AI
- AI benefits achieving today vs hope to achieve:
  - Improve efficiency/productivity: 66% achieving, 60% hope to
  - Enhance decision-making/data insights: 60% achieving, 65% hope to
  - Reduce costs: 53% achieving, 40% hope to
  - Enhance client/customer relationships: 40% achieving, 38% hope to
  - Improve products/services and foster innovation: 38% achieving, 38% hope to
  - Increase revenue: 20% achieving, 74% hope to
- Revenue growth largely remains an aspiration

### Current Approaches to Transformation
- 34% — deep transformation of products, processes, and business models
- 30% — redesigning key processes around AI (business model intact)
- 37% — little or no change to existing processes (surface-level AI use)
- Only the 34% are truly reimaginationg businesses vs optimizing existing

### AI Fluency and Work Redesign
- 84% of companies have NOT redesigned jobs around AI capabilities
- 36% expect at least 10% of jobs fully automated within a year
- 82% expect at least 10% of jobs fully automated within 3 years
- Workers skills cited as biggest barrier to AI integration
- Yet only 53% focus on educating employees for AI fluency
- Fewer than half making significant adjustments to talent strategies
- Talent strategy adjustments:
  - Educating workforce on AI fluency: 53%
  - Designing upskilling/reskilling strategies: 48%
  - Assessing target talent acquisition for specialized talent: 36%
  - Redesigning career paths and mobility strategies: 33%
  - Assessing supply/demand changes for skills: 30%
  - Providing performance-based incentives for AI leverage: 30%
  - Combining or reimagining organizations based on new work patterns: 30%
  - Measuring worker trust and engagement: 30%
  - Changing balance between full-time, contract, gig workers: 19%
- Worker sentiment (non-technical workers):
  - 13% highly enthusiastic and proactively seeking to use AI
  - 55% open to exploring AI
  - 21% prefer not to use AI but will if required
  - 4% actively distrust and avoid it
- 36% expect 10%+ job automation within a year
- Entry-level roles (data entry, reconciliation, first-level support) prioritized for automation
- These are often starting points for longer careers → need alternate advancement pathways
- 53% have considered pod-based/non-hierarchical models (fewer roles require supervision)
- Only 16% moved to such models to great/maximum extent

### Sovereign AI
- 83% view sovereign AI as at least moderately important to strategic planning
- 43% rate it as very important or extremely important
- 77% factor AI solution's country of origin into vendor selection decisions
- 58% build AI stacks primarily with local vendors
- 66% express at least moderate concern about reliance on foreign-owned AI
- 22% very or extremely concerned about reliance on foreign AI
- Americas: only 11% rely on foreign-sourced solutions for majority of AI stack
- EMEA: 32% rely on foreign-sourced solutions for majority of stack
- Sovereign AI = country/companies design, train, deploy AI under own laws, own infrastructure, locally governed data
- About strategic independence, not just technology ownership

### Agentic AI
- 74% of companies plan to deploy agentic AI within two years
- Today: 23% using agentic AI at least moderately
- In 2 years: 74% expect to use agentic AI at least moderately
  - 5% fully integrated, 23% extensive, 46% moderate, 21% minimal, 5% not at all
- Only 21% report having a mature governance model for autonomous agents
- 85% expect to customize agents to fit unique needs
- Agentic AI highest potential in: customer support, supply chain management, R&D, knowledge management, cybersecurity
- AI agents don't eliminate humans — workers become force multipliers, watching agents, handling human-in-the-loop gates
- Companies seeing most success: measured approach, lower-risk use cases first, building governance before scaling
- Cross-functional governance needed: IT, legal, compliance, business unit leaders

### Physical AI
- 58% of companies using physical AI today (at least limited use)
- 80% expect to use physical AI within two years
- 18% leveraging to moderate or greater extent today
- In 2 years: 15% extensive, 3% fully integrated
- Asia Pacific leads: 71% currently using, 90% expect to use in 2 years
  - Americas: 56% currently, 77% in 2 years
  - EMEA: 56% currently, 81% in 2 years
- Leading areas: manufacturing, logistics, defense
- Controlled environments (factories, warehouses) progress faster than open environments
- Types with greatest expected impact:
  - Intelligent security systems/smart monitoring: 21%
  - Collaborative robotics: 20%
  - Digital twins: 19%
  - IoT-driven retail: 16%
  - Autonomous logistics: 13%
  - Smart materials: 7%
- Key barriers: cost (facility retrofits, sensors, robots, integration, maintenance, downtime)
- A warehouse robot project might need hundreds of thousands in AI dev but millions in physical infrastructure
- Higher costs, longer development cycles, stricter safety regulations vs software agentic AI
- Physical AI definition: AI systems that perceive the real world, make decisions, and drive physical actions through machines or control systems

### AI Preparedness (high preparedness)
- Technology infrastructure: 43% (down from 42% last year)
- Strategy: 42% (up +3pp) — highly prepared for AI adoption
- Data management: 40% (down from 44%)
- Risk and governance: 30% (up +6pp)
- Talent: 20% (down from 22%)
- Most respondents believe resolving key AI challenges will take more than a year
- "Nearly 80–90% of new use cases are generative AI. So yes, companies prepared, but for a different future."

### Top AI Risks (most concerning)
- Data privacy and/or security: 73%
- Legal, IP, or regulatory compliance: 50%
- Governance capabilities and oversight: 46%
- Model quality, consistency, and explainability: 46%
- Workforce impact: 30%

### Recommendations (Six Key Focus Areas)
1. **Close the gap between access and activation** — focus on system integration, data permissions, operational reliability, design for deployment from the start, hands-on role-specific training, visible executive advocacy
2. **Unlock human advantage by redesigning work around AI** — reimagine jobs combining human strengths and AI capabilities, streamline workflows AI can execute end-to-end, humans focus on judgment/exception handling/strategic oversight, AI-native approach rather than layering on legacy processes
3. **Build governance before scale, make it everyone's role** — governance as catalyst for growth, not compliance exercise, senior leadership must actively shape AI governance, cross-functional teams (tech, legal, compliance, business), balance risk management with innovation
4. **Address sovereign AI requirements** — assess data and workloads that must remain within boundaries, determine where local model hosting is mandatory, establish policies for data residency, model retraining, cross-border flows
5. **Build a "living" technology and data infrastructure** — (more details in second half)
6. **Pursue strategic reinvention** — (more details in second half)

### Additional Insights
- "Many organizations prepared for an AI future by building infrastructure and governance for traditional AI models. With LLMs, those efforts were upended."
- Companies are merging technology and people-leadership functions
- Governance should be calibrated to enable experimentation, not constrain it

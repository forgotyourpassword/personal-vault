## Current Reading

- *Project Hail Mary* — Andy Weir — 80% complete
  - A lone astronaut wakes up with no memory on a mission to save Earth from an extinction-level threat. The story blends hard science problem-solving with humor, isolation, and an unlikely partnership. It is ultimately about ingenuity, sacrifice, and staying useful when the stakes are absurdly high.
- *Steve Jobs* — Walter Isaacson — 30% complete
  - This biography follows Steve Jobs from his early life through Apple, Pixar, NeXT, and his return to Apple. It shows both the brilliance and damage of his intensity, taste, control, and reality-distortion field. The useful thread is how product obsession, narrative, and standards can reshape entire industries, while also costing real human collateral.

## Finished

- 2026-05-08 — *The Giver* — Lois Lowry
  - A boy named Jonas lives in a controlled society that has eliminated pain, conflict, and deep emotion by suppressing memory and choice. When he becomes the Receiver of Memory, he learns what his community sacrificed for sameness and safety. The book is a clean, sharp argument that freedom, love, and truth require accepting discomfort and risk.

## Want to Read

- *Influence* — Robert Cialdini
  - Cialdini explains the core psychological principles that make people say yes, including reciprocity, commitment, social proof, liking, authority, and scarcity. The book is practical because it shows these forces in everyday sales, negotiation, marketing, and decision-making. It is also a defensive manual for noticing when those same levers are being pulled on you.
- *Pre-Suasion* — Robert Cialdini
  - This book focuses on what happens before a message is delivered and how attention shapes receptivity. Cialdini argues that the context, framing, and mental state created before a request can determine whether persuasion succeeds. The main lesson is that influence often starts upstream, before the actual pitch begins.
- *Yes! 50 Scientifically Proven Ways to Be Persuasive* — Noah J. Goldstein, Steve J. Martin, and Robert Cialdini
  - This book turns persuasion research into fifty short, practical tactics. Each chapter explains a specific behavioral-science finding and how it applies to communication, sales, leadership, or marketing. It is likely useful as a tactical reference rather than a deep theory book.
- *Expert Secrets* — Russell Brunson
  - Brunson lays out how to package expertise into a message, movement, and offer that people want to follow. The book emphasizes storytelling, audience identity, belief-building, and sales funnels. Its practical value is in turning knowledge into a marketable framework without making the offer feel like generic commodity advice.
- *One to Many* — Jason Fladlien
  - Fladlien focuses on selling through webinars and presentations where one speaker influences many buyers at once. The book breaks down structure, teaching, objection handling, and offer timing. Its core promise is that a strong presentation can educate, build trust, and sell at scale without relying only on one-on-one selling.
- *Ready, Fire, Aim* — Michael Masterson
  - Masterson argues that businesses need different priorities at different stages of revenue growth. Early on, speed, selling, and offer-market fit matter more than polished operations or perfect planning. The book is useful for keeping entrepreneurial energy aimed at revenue instead of getting trapped in premature optimization.
- *Wooden on Leadership* — John Wooden
  - Wooden distills his leadership philosophy from decades of coaching, centered on character, preparation, discipline, and standards. The book emphasizes building people and systems before obsessing over outcomes. Its value is a steady model of leadership rooted in consistency rather than charisma or hype.
- *The Bezos Letters* — Steve Anderson and Karen Anderson
  - This book extracts business principles from Jeff Bezos’s Amazon shareholder letters. It focuses on risk-taking, customer obsession, experimentation, long-term thinking, and scalable systems. The useful angle is seeing how Amazon’s operating philosophy can be translated into repeatable business habits.
- *The Sales Acceleration Formula* — Mark Roberge
  - Roberge describes how HubSpot built a scalable sales engine using data, process, hiring profiles, and repeatable training. The book treats sales less like charisma and more like an engineering problem. Its best use is for thinking about how to build measurable, coachable, and predictable sales capacity.
- *Predictable Revenue* — Aaron Ross and Marylou Tyler
  - This book explains the outbound sales model that helped Salesforce create a repeatable revenue machine. It separates prospecting, closing, and account management into distinct roles and processes. The core idea is that predictable pipeline comes from specialized systems, not heroic individual sellers.
- *The Motive* — Patrick Lencioni
  - Lencioni contrasts reward-centered leadership with responsibility-centered leadership. Through a business fable, he argues that many executives avoid the uncomfortable work only leaders can do, such as managing teams, having hard conversations, and creating clarity. The book is a gut-check on whether someone wants leadership for service and responsibility or for status.

Maryland separation agreement drafting guide for
professional services firms
A Baltimore-based outsourced accounting and advisory firm must navigate a complex
overlay of federal and Maryland-specific rules to produce a separation agreement that
is both enforceable and compliant. This reference document covers every major clause,
prohibition, OWBPA requirement, tax structure, and consideration issue the firm will
encounter. The guidance reflects law current through April 2026 and flags every area where
changes occurred after January 2024. Throughout, provisions that touch client financial
data, proprietary methodology, and direct client relationships receive special attention.
Every separation agreement produced from this template should be reviewed by a
licensed Maryland employment attorney before use. The areas flagged for mandatory
attorney review include OWBPA group-termination disclosures, restrictive covenant
enforceability, Section 409A payment timing, and any provisions touching trade secrets or
whistleblower rights.

Consideration

The consideration clause is the engine of the release. Under both Maryland common law
and the OWBPA (29 U.S.C. § 626(f)(1)(D)), the payment must be something of value the
employee is not already owed. Final wages, accrued PTO (where owed under § 3-505),
and earned commissions are existing obligations — not consideration. The agreement must
bifurcate these from the new severance payment. Failure to do so is the single most
common reason Maryland courts scrutinize releases. The consideration clause should
contain an explicit acknowledgment: “The Severance Payment exceeds anything of value to
which Employee is already entitled.”

TOPIC 2: Provisions that will sink the agreement
Overbroad waivers of future claims are void
The OWBPA flatly prohibits waiving claims that “may arise after the date the waiver is
executed” (29 U.S.C. § 626(f)(1)(C)).

UpCounsel

Whitmanlegalsolutions

Catch-all language

releasing claims “past, present, or future” voids the ADEA waiver and invites challenges
under other statutes. Best practice: Limit the release to claims “arising on or before the
Effective Date of this Agreement.”

EEOC and MCCR charge-filing rights cannot be waived
An employee can waive the right to personal monetary recovery from a charge but cannot
waive the right to file a charge or participate in an investigation. This distinction was
established in EEOC v. Cosmair, Inc., 821 F.2d 1085, 1090–91 (5th Cir. 1987), and is codified
for ADEA waivers at 29 U.S.C. § 626(f)(4). The EEOC’s Enforcement Guidance No. 915.002
(April 10, 1997) confirms that promises not to file charges are “null and void as a matter of
public policy.”

TOPIC 5: What counts as consideration and how to protect it
Maryland courts enforce releases but require real bargained-for value
Maryland follows the standard common-law rule: consideration must be present, but courts
generally will not evaluate its adequacy. Blumenthal v. Heron, 261 Md. 234, 274 A.2d 636

for a promise so long as it has some value.”

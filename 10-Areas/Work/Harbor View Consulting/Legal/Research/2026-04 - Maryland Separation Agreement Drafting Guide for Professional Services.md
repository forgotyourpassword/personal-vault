## What this is
A Maryland-specific separation agreement drafting guide tailored for professional services firms, covering federal and Maryland-specific rules through April 2026. Prepared for a Baltimore-based outsourced accounting and advisory firm context.

## Important caution
The document itself states that every separation agreement produced from this guidance should be reviewed by a licensed Maryland employment attorney before use. Areas flagged as mandatory attorney review include OWBPA group-termination disclosures, restrictive covenant enforceability, Section 409A payment timing, and provisions touching trade secrets or whistleblower rights.

## Topics covered

### Topic 1: Essential clauses
- Termination details: state last day of active work, formal separation date, characterization (voluntary/involuntary), reference-disclosure policy
- MWPCL obligation: all wages must be paid on the next regular payday after termination — unconditional, unaffected by the agreement
- Consideration: must be bifurcated; final wages, accrued PTO (where owed), and earned commissions cannot be bundled with severance consideration
- General release: enumerate federal and Maryland statutes by name; includes Title VII, ADA, ADEA (must be named specifically under OWBPA), FMLA, EPA, Section 1981, GINA, ERISA (vested benefits cannot be released), Maryland FEPA (updated October 2024 to add military status), MWPCL, Maryland Equal Pay for Equal Work Act
- Mandatory carve-outs: EEOC/MCCR charge-filing, NLRB Section 7 activity, SEC/IRS whistleblower reporting and awards, workers' compensation
- COBRA and benefits: federal COBRA (18 months for 20+ employee firms); Maryland mini-COBRA for firms under 20 (45-day election window, unavailable for terminations "for cause"); COBRA notice must be provided independently, not conditioned on signing; failure to provide timely notice triggers $100/day excise tax under IRC § 4980B
- Return of property and trade secrets: must require return of all physical and electronic property; written certification that all electronic copies including BYOD and cloud storage have been deleted; must include DTSA whistleblower immunity notice (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)); omitting it forfeits exemplary damages and attorney's fees under DTSA
- Cooperation: define scope, set a time limit (typically 24 months), compensate for anything beyond de minimis threshold (e.g., 10 hours at a stated rate)
- Severability, governing law, integration: standard

### Topic 2: Prohibited provisions and high-risk traps
- FLSA claims cannot be privately released (Brooklyn Savings Bank v. O'Neil, 1945; Lynn's Food Stores, 11th Cir. 1982; Johnson v. CRC Holdings, D. Md. — extended to MWPCL as well). Resolution requires DOL-supervised settlement or court-approved stipulated judgment.
- Workers' compensation: private release is unenforceable; must be approved by the Maryland Workers' Compensation Commission under Md. Code Ann., Lab. & Empl. § 9-722
- SEC Rule 21F-17(a): prohibits any action impeding SEC communication about securities violations. Seven companies settled with SEC in September 2024 for over $3 million combined for requiring employees to waive whistleblower awards. Even private companies are subject. The violation is the language itself — no actual chilling need be shown.
- Non-disparagement: use "knowingly false or defamatory statements" — not broad language prohibiting critical commentary; narrow confidentiality to trade secrets and proprietary information, not the agreement's existence or terms
- NLRA Section 7 savings clause required
- McLaren Macomb noted as binding Board precedent despite GC memo rescission

### Topic 3: OWBPA — the eight strict requirements
OWBPA (29 U.S.C. § 626(f)) is strict and unqualified. Burden of proof is on the employer. Requirements:
1. Plain language calculated to be understood
2. Specific reference to the ADEA by name (a generic "all federal claims" release is insufficient per Oubre v. Entergy)
3. No waiver of future claims
4. Additional consideration beyond existing entitlements
5. Written advisement to consult an attorney (passive acknowledgment of opportunity is insufficient)
6. 21-day consideration period for individual terminations; 45 days for group/exit-incentive programs. Material changes restart the clock.
7. 7-day revocation period (cannot be shortened). Agreement becomes effective Day 8. No severance before that date.
8. For group terminations: written disclosure of decisional unit, eligibility factors, time limits, and a chart of job titles and ages for selected and non-selected employees. Decisional-unit identification is where most group waivers fail (Kruchowski v. Weyerhaeuser, 10th Cir. 2006; Adams v. Moore Bus. Forms, 4th Cir. 2000 — controlling for Maryland).

Critical rule: A defective waiver is void. The employee can pursue ADEA claims while keeping the severance (Oubre). There is no cure.

### Topic 4: Payment structure, taxes, and unemployment
- Federal income tax: supplemental wages at flat 22% up to $1M; 37% above $1M (made permanent by P.L. 119-21). Maryland has no separate flat withholding rate.
- FICA: severance is wages under Quality Stores (U.S. Supreme Court, 2014). Social Security at 6.2% up to $184,500 wage base (2026). Medicare at 1.45% uncapped; Additional Medicare Tax at 0.9% on wages exceeding $200,000.
- Maryland state tax: 2% to 6.50% progressive brackets (two higher brackets added Budget Reconciliation and Financing Act of 2025). Baltimore City local income tax: 3.20%.
- Combined marginal rate for a Baltimore City resident can reach approximately 46.7% federal + state + local before FICA, or roughly 54% effective including FICA.
- Maryland unemployment: salary continuation always offsets unemployment benefits week-for-week. Lump-sum severance when position is genuinely eliminated does NOT offset benefits. Lump-sum when position is NOT eliminated does offset. Structural choice matters.
- Section 409A: two key exemptions — short-term deferral (payment by March 15 of year following year separation risk ends) and separation-pay exception (total severance does not exceed 2x lesser of prior-year compensation or § 401(a)(17) limit of $360,000 for 2026, yielding $720,000 cap; paid by December 31 of second year following separation). These can be stacked. For December terminations with a 21-day consideration period running into January: agreement must specify payment occurs in the later calendar year or on a fixed date after release period ends, or impermissible employee discretion under 409A is created.
- Six-month specified-employee delay for public companies does not apply to privately held firms.

### Topic 5: Consideration
- Maryland courts enforce releases but require real bargained-for value. Blumenthal v. Heron (Md. 1971): courts will not inquire into adequacy of consideration so long as it has some value.
- However: Blum v. Blum (Md. App. 1984): court may set aside agreements that are "so unfair and inequitable" especially where confidential relationship existed or duress alleged.
- Cheek v. United Healthcare (Md. 2003): illusory promise (leaving promisor free to perform or not at sole discretion) cannot constitute consideration.
- Already-owed amounts are non-negotiable: final wages, accrued PTO (where policy requires), earned commissions (Medex v. McCabe, Md. 2002) — conditioning any of these on signing a release violates MWPCL and exposes firm to treble damages and attorney's fees (§ 3-507.2(b)).
- Must have two clearly separated sections: (A) amounts already owed paid unconditionally regardless of signing; (B) additional severance consideration paid only upon execution and expiration of revocation period.
- Common structural failure: bundling these two sections. Creates no independent consideration for release and invites coercion/unconscionability arguments.
- No statutory minimum for consideration, but nominal consideration is reckless in the employment context. Best practice: one to two weeks' additional pay per year of service, or a practical minimum of two weeks' additional pay beyond all amounts owed.
- New Maryland obligations to note: SB 525/HB 649 wage-transparency requirements (effective October 1, 2024); Maryland FAMLI program (contributions begin July 1, 2025; benefits begin July 1, 2026).

## The four non-negotiable rules stated in this guide
1. Bifurcate consideration ruthlessly
2. Treat OWBPA compliance as binary — the eight requirements are strict and unqualified
3. Include every required carve-out — penalties for omission exceed any marginal confidentiality benefit
4. Structure payments to match business objectives, with careful 409A analysis for installment plans

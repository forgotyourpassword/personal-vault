# A Players Without Ruthlessness

Captured: 2026-05-16
Type: Leadership / people thought
Related reading: [[Steve Jobs]] / Walter Isaacson

## Source thought

While reading the Steve Jobs biography, Michael noted Jobs's belief that building a team of A players required ruthlessness. Jobs's logic was that A players only like to work with A players, which means leaders cannot indulge B players.

The specific trigger was Jobs firing roughly 25% of the Lisa team after saying they had failed and calling them B and C players.

Michael's reaction:

> I don't know what he meant by ruthless, but I don't think that's how I'd want to be described.

## Why this matters

The useful idea is not that Harbor View should copy Jobs's harshness. The useful tension is this:

- High performers want to work with other high performers.
- Letting underperformance linger is unfair to the people carrying the standard.
- But "ruthless" can become a permission slip for arrogance, cruelty, impatience, or lazy people judgment.

The better Harbor View version is probably **high standards without contempt**.

That means being clear, direct, and fast about fit and performance, but not dehumanizing people as "B" or "C" players. The standard can be serious without the leader needing to be brutal.

## Operating principle

Do not indulge chronic underperformance, but do not make ruthlessness the identity.

A cleaner phrasing:

> We protect A-player teams by making expectations explicit, coaching early, deciding honestly, and moving people out of wrong seats with dignity when the fit is not there.

## Possible Harbor View moves

- Define what "A-player" means by seat, not by vague personal judgment.
- Use scorecards and role expectations to separate performance evidence from personality labels.
- Coach early when someone is drifting below standard.
- Make the decision point explicit: improve, change seats, or exit.
- Protect strong performers from carrying avoidable drag indefinitely.
- Avoid calling people B/C players as an identity. Talk about fit, standards, behavior, and results.

## Guardrails

- Do not confuse kindness with avoiding hard calls.
- Do not confuse high standards with public shaming or contempt.
- Do not use "A player" language as ego decoration.
- Do not let one person's brilliance excuse destructive behavior.
- Do not let compassion become a hiding place for indecision.

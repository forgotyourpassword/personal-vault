# Agentic AI Adoption

## Definition
AI agents that can set goals, reason through multi-step tasks, use tools and APIs, and coordinate work with people or other agents. This is a shift from AI as a source of information/insights to AI as a system that performs work.

## Adoption Trajectory
| Extent | Today | In 2 Years |
|---|---|---|
| Not at all | 52% | 5% |
| Minimal | 25% | 21% |
| Moderate | 20% | 46% |
| Extensive | 2% | 23% |
| Fully integrated | 1% | 5% |

**23%** of companies using agentic AI at least moderately today; **74%** expect to within two years.

## High-Impact Use Cases
1. Customer support (highest impact expected)
2. Supply chain management
3. R&D
4. Knowledge management
5. Cybersecurity

## Real-World Deployments
- **Financial services** — Agentic workflows capture meeting actions from video conferences, draft communications, track follow-through
- **Aviation** — AI agents handle common transactions (rebooking flights, rerouting bags), freeing humans for complex matters
- **Manufacturing** — AI agents support new product development, balance cost vs time-to-market
- **Public sector** — AI agents cover workforce shortages, partner with humans for key processes

## The Human Factor
Agentic AI doesn't eliminate humans — it creates new demands for uniquely human strengths (adaptivity, judgment). Workers become "force multipliers" watching agents, checking metrics, and providing human-in-the-loop accountability. The transition creates **more work initially** for existing staff as they learn to monitor and manage autonomous systems.

## Governance Gap
Only **21%** of companies report having a mature governance model for autonomous agents. Companies seeing the most success:
1. Start with lower-risk use cases
2. Build governance capabilities first
3. Scale deliberately

## Customization
85% of companies expect to customize agents to fit unique business needs — out-of-the-box agent solutions won't meet most organizations' requirements.
